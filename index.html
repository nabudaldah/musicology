Sound

In nature, anything that vibrates pressurizes gasses in the air and these tiny pressurizations can be perceived by our ears and is interpreted by our brains. When a string of any material is tensioned, it vibrates in a very orderly way. The way it moves generates a vibration that we call a sine wave. While no sound in nature is purely sinusoidal, all sounds are made up of combinations of pure sine waves that are added together!

Pitch

A perfect sine wave has a certain pitch. That is the frequency or the speed of its vibration per second expressed in Hertz and abbreviated as Hz. Both the frequency as the labelling of pitches is cultural and has changed over time and place. The human hearing system can typically perceive vibration between 20Hz and 20,000Hz. Human voice typically ranges between 85Hz and 255Hz.

Octaves

Some pitches sound pleasant to us humans. One of the pitches that go well together are pitches that are exact multiples of each other. Any pitch that is doubled in frequency sounds well with it's base frequency.

Somehow, we perceive this as being the same tone. This relationship is called an octave and is universal across cultures. However, this simple ratio of 1 to 2 is only one of the many ratios that sound great!

Harmonics

The doubling of a pitch is already a harmonic. Tripling a pitch is also a harmonic! But multiplying a pitch by 3:2 or 4:3 and 5:3 are all harmonics of that pitch. These simple ratio multiplications of a pitch sound pleasing together and they are called the harmonic series.

For still unknown reason, we find these simple ratios between pitches very pleasant, while we feel tension when these ratios are more "complicated" ratios, such as 89:53 or 241:479.

These simple multiples of the pitch, or simple mathematical ratios of the pitch, sounds great together and are the fundamentals of notes, scales and chords that we use to make music that sounds great to us!

Resources: Andre Huang, Ableton Learn, Karl Casey, Adventures in Music Theory

Chromatic Scale

Historically in Western music, 12 pitches have been used to make music. These 12 pitches are together called the chromatic scale. While 12 discrete pitches are arbitrary and cultural, there is some foundation as to how it got to the number 12, given the harmonic series that sound great together: there are a few well spread out pitches that have simple ratios between them. But as you see, there are some big gaps! Especially between A and B there is nothing. To make these pitch jumps a little smaller, in-between notes where invented with less simple ratios.

Doesn't this look oddly familiar? Yes it does! It's the piano keys. The white and black keys. In practice however, tuning instruments isn't done using these simple ratios, but rather the twelve equal temperament tuning system.

The piano in C has become the "QWERTY" layout of music input devices. But beware, you do not need to use a classical piano keys layout to make music! Below are some alternatives that are more straight forward.

Tuning

Tuning mechanical devices, such as piano's and guitars, to consistently reproduce exact pitches, is a complicated and cumbersome business, especially when having many octaves of tones to reproduce for a whole orchestra! Therefore the equal temperament tuning was invented with A4 at 440Hz. The logarithmic scale comes very close to the simple ratios that have made their way into the chromatic scale.

Using this method, each note has the exact same distance in terms of pitch ratio to any other note. This makes every instrument a little "out of tune" compared to the simple pitch ratios, but it is acceptable for the benefit it provides.

Chords

While electronic instruments have the ability to generate many types of lush sounds, on classical instruments we can play multiple notes at the same time to make them sound fuller, richer and more lush. Playing the different notes at the same time on the same instrument is called playing a chord.

The most well-known chords are the major triads and minor triads. These are called triads as one is said to play three notes at the same time. The major triad is playing the unison, the major third and the fifth at the same time.

The minor triad is playing the unison, the minor third and the fifth. But you can play any number of notes at the same time to your liking. Once that sound great have a simple harmonic relationship though!
